DNA Detective Work Cracks the Code of Salamander Love Lives

By Bear Lee · February 6, 2026 · 5 min read

Deep beneath the Korean peninsula, a mystery has been quietly unfolding for years. Scientists have been scratching their heads (and probably other body parts) trying to figure out exactly when Korean clawed salamanders decide to get romantic. Well, mystery solved – and the answer is more interesting than anyone expected.

According to breakthrough research published in PLOS ONE, these subterranean creatures don't just breed once a year like many of their amphibian cousins. Instead, Korean clawed salamanders (Onychodactylus koreanus) have a biannual breeding season – basically, they're twice as busy in the love department as scientists previously thought.

The real game-changer here is how researchers cracked this biological code. Instead of trying to chase these elusive underground dwellers around with tiny nets (which sounds both adorable and futile), scientists used environmental DNA analysis. Think of it as genetic detective work – they could identify and study the salamanders by analyzing traces of DNA left behind in their environment.

This cutting-edge approach, combined with population assessment techniques, finally revealed the species' true life cycle patterns. For a creature that spends most of its time hidden away from prying human eyes, having two breeding seasons per year suggests these salamanders have been living much more active social lives than anyone realized.

The research represents a significant step forward in understanding these fascinating subterranean creatures. By solving this long-standing mystery, scientists now have a clearer picture of how Korean clawed salamanders organize their underground communities – and apparently, they're quite the social butterflies. Or should we say, social salamanders?

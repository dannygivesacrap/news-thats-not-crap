North Atlantic Right Whales See Best Birth Season in Years

By Wanda Whale · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

With only about 370-384 individuals left on Earth, the North Atlantic right whale doesn't have much margin for error. But after reaching a low of 358 individuals in 2020, the population is showing signs of gradual recovery—and the current calving season is delivering even more hope.

Researchers monitoring the calving grounds have confirmed 21 calves so far in the current 2025-2026 calving season. This follows the 2025 season, when 11 calves were born, including four from first-time mothers. For a species where every single birth matters, this is exactly what recovery looks like when it starts.

In October 2025, NOAA Fisheries estimated that there were 384 (+10/-9) individual North Atlantic right whales alive at the start of 2024—representing a 2.1 percent increase in population size from the 2023 estimate of 376. After years of decline, the upward trend is encouraging.

"Every calf is a small miracle. Twenty-one calves this season? That's twenty-one small miracles swimming along the Atlantic coast right now."

North Atlantic right whales got their name because whalers considered them the "right" whale to hunt—they're slow, float when killed, and produce abundant oil and baleen. By the time commercial whaling ended in the 1930s, the population had been decimated to fewer than 100 individuals.

Why This Matters

Right whale reproduction is a slow, uncertain business. Females don't reach sexual maturity until around 10 years old, pregnancy lasts a year, and mothers typically wait 5-10 years between calves. There are approximately 70 reproductively active females remaining. Climate change has made things worse by shifting their food supply—tiny copepods—to new locations, forcing whales to travel farther and expend more energy.

The result has been a reproduction crisis. Between 2017 and 2020, the population saw an average of just 4 calves per year—nowhere near enough to offset deaths. The species was on track for functional extinction within decades.

The recent calving seasons suggest something may be changing. The 2025 season's 11 calves, with four first-time mothers in the group, was an encouraging sign. The final pair was spotted on April 17, 2025—an unusually late sighting. Two mothers were even spotted with their calves in unexpected places: one off New York in February and another in Cape Cod Bay in April.

The Threats Haven't Gone Away

Since 2017, the whales have been experiencing an Unusual Mortality Event, which has resulted in more than 20 percent of the population being sick, injured, or killed. The primary causes are entanglements in fishing gear and collisions with boats and ships.

But here's the good news: in 2025, there were no detected right whale mortalities. It's undoubtedly positive news, and underscores the need for continued protection of the species range-wide.

"When ships slow down, whales survive. It's really that simple," conservationists note. The shipping industry has been more cooperative, and vessel speed restrictions in calving areas appear to be working.

What Comes Next

The calves now face their first migration—a 1,000-mile journey up the Atlantic coast to feeding grounds in the Gulf of Maine and Canada. It's a treacherous trip through some of the busiest shipping lanes on Earth, past countless lobster and crab pot lines.

Scientists at the New England Aquarium emphasize that protections remain essential to continue the population's gradual increase. New regulations requiring weaker ropes in fishing gear and expanded vessel speed zones will help protect these calves as they grow.

"Ask me in 10 years if these calves made it to adulthood, and then I'll tell you if this was a turning point," researchers say. "But for today? For right now? We're going to enjoy the fact that there are baby whales swimming around out there, learning to be whales. That's worth celebrating."

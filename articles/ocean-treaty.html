UN High Seas Treaty Enters Into Force—A Historic Day for Ocean Protection

By Marina Waters · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

On January 17, 2026, the United Nations High Seas Treaty—formally known as the Agreement on Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction (BBNJ)—officially entered into force. After nearly two decades of negotiations and 18 months of rapid ratifications, international waters finally have legal protection.

Let's talk about the high seas, because most people have no idea how vast they actually are. Once you sail past 200 nautical miles from any coastline, you enter international waters—areas that belong to no nation and have, until now, been governed by essentially no one.

These aren't small patches of ocean. The high seas cover roughly 64% of the ocean's surface and nearly half of Earth's total area. They're home to countless species, many still undiscovered. And for decades, they existed in a regulatory void—open to exploitation by anyone with a ship big enough to get there.

"This is a victory for multilateralism and for global efforts to counter the destructive trends facing ocean health."

Those words come from UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres, who praised the treaty's entry into force as a "historic achievement." The agreement was adopted in June 2023 after nearly 20 years of negotiations, then opened for signatures in September 2023. It reached the required 60 ratifications in September 2025.

What the Treaty Actually Does

The BBNJ Agreement establishes four crucial mechanisms. First, it creates a framework for establishing Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) in international waters—something previously impossible because no entity had the authority to designate them.

Second, it mandates environmental impact assessments for activities in international waters, from deep-sea mining to commercial fishing. Companies and countries can no longer just show up and start extracting without demonstrating they won't cause irreparable harm.

Third, it establishes rules for the equitable sharing of benefits from marine genetic resources. The ocean contains countless organisms with potential pharmaceutical and industrial applications, and now developing nations will share in those benefits, not just wealthy countries with research fleets.

Fourth, it includes provisions for capacity building and technology transfer to help developing nations participate fully in high seas research and conservation.

How We Got Here

International treaties are notoriously difficult to negotiate. The BBNJ process began formally in 2004 with a UN working group, followed by preparatory committees starting in 2016 and formal negotiations from 2018 to 2023. Different nations had vastly different interests—rich nations wanted resource access, developing nations wanted fair benefit-sharing, fishing nations worried about restrictions.

The breakthrough came at 4:30 AM on March 4, 2023, in New York, when exhausted negotiators finally reached agreement after a 36-hour marathon session. Rena Lee, Singapore's Ambassador for Oceans and Law of the Sea, who presided over the negotiations, called it "the ship has reached the shore."

The EU and its member states led the ratification push, with the bloc becoming the 60th party in September 2025—exactly the number needed to bring the treaty into force 120 days later on January 17, 2026.

What Happens Next

The treaty is now law, but implementation will take years. The first Conference of the Parties (COP) will convene within one year to establish operational details. Marine Protected Areas need to be proposed and designated. Monitoring and enforcement mechanisms need to be created. Scientific bodies need to be established.

But the foundation is in place. For the first time, humanity has a comprehensive legal framework for protecting the vast majority of the ocean. As Greenpeace ocean campaigner Laura Meller put it: "We've spent 20 years getting here. Now the real work begins."

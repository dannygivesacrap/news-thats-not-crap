Coral Reefs Could Help Solve Global Hunger

By Coral Reefson · January 29, 2026 · 6 min read

We usually hear about coral reefs in the context of climate doom. But research from the Smithsonian Tropical Research Institute shows these underwater cities might be one of our best tools for feeding a hungry planet—if we let fish populations recover.

Here's a number that doesn't get enough attention: coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor but support roughly 25% of all marine species. More importantly for the nearly 1 billion humans who depend on reef fish for protein, reefs are fish factories of extraordinary efficiency—when they're allowed to function.

Scientists at STRI and collaborators have found that allowing coral reef fish populations to recover could dramatically increase the number of sustainable fish servings produced per year. Their research reveals that many reef fish populations are so depleted that they are producing far less than their sustainable potential.

"We've been treating reef fisheries like ATMs—just withdrawing without ever checking the balance. Turns out, if you manage the account properly, there's a lot more in there than we thought."

The study shows that coral reefs worldwide could increase sustainable fish yields by nearly 50% if fish populations were allowed to recover. This recovery could provide countries with 20,000 up to 162 million additional sustainable servings—enough to meet recommended seafood intake for several million people annually.

Who Benefits Most?

The largest gains would occur in countries already facing serious hunger and micronutrient deficiencies. Regions in Africa and Southeast Asia stand to benefit the most from reef recovery. Indonesia, in particular, was identified as having the greatest potential increase in food supply from restored reef fisheries.

Degradation of coral reef island ecosystems greatly affects nutritious and safe food availability, compromising food security. Reefs are often termed "Sea Supermarkets" ensuring the availability of crucial proteins and livelihoods for communities residing in coastal areas.

Countries with high hunger and nutrient deficiencies would benefit the most, and rebuilding reefs could turn ocean conservation into a powerful tool against global hunger.

Global Investment in Reef Recovery

By the end of 2024, the Global Fund for Coral Reefs (GFCR) was active in more than 20 reef nations, backing over 150 reef-positive enterprises and finance solutions. The investments have directly improved the livelihoods of over 30,000 people, with nearly 50% of new jobs filled by women.

In early 2025, the Coast3D Reefs project marked a major milestone with the installation of three custom-designed reefs in the Red Sea. Each reef, standing 1.7 meters tall and covering 15 square meters, was carefully assembled underwater by a dive team.

In total, more than 4.15 million people have benefited through improved food security, ecosystem services, and economic resilience from GFCR-supported projects.

Climate Change: The Elephant in the Lagoon

Between January 2023 and May 2025, 84% of coral reefs worldwide were exposed to bleaching-level heat stress—marking the Fourth Global Coral Bleaching Event, the most extensive and severe ever recorded. The 2025 Global Tipping Points Report confirms that coral reefs are now among the first ecosystems to pass a global tipping point.

But well-managed reefs appear to be more resilient to climate impacts. Fish populations that haven't been decimated by overfishing help control algae that can smother heat-stressed corals. Diverse, healthy ecosystems recover faster from bleaching events.

Better management isn't a substitute for climate action. But it's the best way to keep reefs alive long enough for us to get our act together on emissions. And in the meantime, it feeds people.

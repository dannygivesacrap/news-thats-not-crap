France Bans PFAS "Forever Chemicals" in Clothing, Cookware, and Cosmetics

By Claire Waters · January 29, 2026 · 4 min read

France has become the first country in the world to implement a comprehensive ban on PFAS—the notorious "forever chemicals"—in everyday consumer products. The landmark legislation, which took effect January 1, 2026, prohibits PFAS in clothing, cookware, and cosmetics, marking a major victory for public health advocates.

PFAS—per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances—are called "forever chemicals" for a grimly accurate reason: they essentially never break down in the environment. Once released, they persist for thousands of years. Once they enter your body through contaminated water, food, or consumer products, they accumulate in your blood, organs, and tissues.

These chemicals are found in everything from non-stick cookware to waterproof jackets to mascara. Scientists have linked PFAS exposure to thyroid disease, liver damage, immune system dysfunction, certain cancers, and reproductive problems. Blood tests consistently find PFAS in 98% of Americans tested—and the numbers are similar across Europe.

"France is sending a clear message: the health of our citizens comes before corporate convenience. These chemicals have no place in our daily lives."

Those words come from Christophe Bechu, France's Minister of Ecological Transition, who championed the legislation through the National Assembly. The law, known as Proposition de loi visant a proteger la population des risques lies aux PFAS, passed on February 27, 2025, with strong cross-party support.

What the Ban Actually Covers

The French law takes a phased approach. As of January 1, 2026, PFAS are banned in cosmetics and ski wax. Starting January 1, 2027, the ban extends to clothing and textiles. On January 1, 2028, cookware and kitchen utensils must be PFAS-free.

The law includes exemptions for medical devices and certain industrial applications where no alternatives currently exist, but manufacturers must actively work toward PFAS-free solutions. Violations can result in fines up to 100,000 euros and product recalls.

"We're not asking companies to do the impossible," said Bechu. "We're giving them time to adapt while making clear that the era of forever chemicals in consumer products is ending."

From Local Crisis to National Action

The legislation gained momentum after PFAS contamination scandals in several French regions. In Pierre-Benite, near Lyon, a chemical plant operated by Arkema has contaminated groundwater and soil for decades. Local residents discovered elevated PFAS levels in their blood, triggering protests and demands for action.

Environmental groups like Generations Futures and the Collectif PFAS mobilized public support, while scientific studies from INSERM (the French National Institute of Health and Medical Research) provided evidence of health impacts. The French Senate's cross-party Environment Committee pushed for comprehensive legislation.

"This law exists because ordinary citizens refused to accept being poisoned," said Francois Veillerette, director of Generations Futures. "It proves democracy can still work when people organize."

Ripple Effects Across Europe

France's ban is expected to accelerate PFAS restrictions across the EU. A proposal submitted by Germany, the Netherlands, Denmark, Norway, and Sweden to the European Chemicals Agency would ban all PFAS except for essential uses—the most comprehensive restriction ever proposed under EU chemical regulations.

Meanwhile, several US states including California, Maine, Minnesota, and Washington have implemented their own PFAS restrictions. The EPA has also proposed drinking water limits for several PFAS compounds.

For French consumers, the change will be gradual but real. That non-stick pan? Soon it'll be ceramic-coated. That waterproof jacket? Plant-based water repellents. That foundation? PFAS-free formula. Small changes, perhaps, but ones that will keep forever chemicals out of French bodies—forever.

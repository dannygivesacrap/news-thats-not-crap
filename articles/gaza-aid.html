Gaza Ceasefire Enabled Massive Aid Surge

By Hope Delivers · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

When the ceasefire took effect on January 19, 2025, WFP trucks immediately crossed into Gaza carrying ready-to-eat meals and bags of wheat flour. Over the following 42 days, the agency delivered over 88 million pounds of food—more than double the monthly average from late 2024—reaching 1.3 million people with lifesaving assistance.

The January 2025 ceasefire represented a critical turning point. Before it took effect, only 166 trucks carrying 2,257 metric tons of food had entered Gaza that month. Once crossings opened, that figure jumped to 600-700 trucks per day. WFP flooded Gaza with aid through all available corridors from Jordan, Israel, and Egypt.

"In the first week of the ceasefire, WFP reached more than 330,000 people with food boxes, hot meals and cash assistance," the agency reported. Through Zikim and Kerem Shalom crossings, over 22 million pounds of food entered Gaza in that first week alone.

"For the first time in months, families in Gaza received significantly more rations—two food boxes and a 55-pound bag of wheat flour. WFP facilitated nearly 40 percent of all humanitarian aid that entered Gaza during the ceasefire."

Three weeks into the ceasefire, WFP had distributed food parcels to one million people in the Gaza Strip against a target of 1.6 million. The scale-up demonstrated what was possible when crossings remained open.

The Scale of the Crisis

The humanitarian context remains dire. In August 2025, the Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) confirmed that famine had taken hold in Gaza City—all three thresholds of catastrophic hunger, acute malnutrition, and mass death had been officially surpassed.

The child malnutrition crisis is particularly severe. During the first two weeks of December 2025, at least 2,407 children were admitted for treatment of acute malnutrition. In all of 2025, some 90,000 children diagnosed with acute malnutrition were admitted for treatment—more than double the 40,000 cases recorded in 2024.

UNRWA teams remain on the ground to deliver aid and provide services to a population overwhelmed by over 15 months of constant bombardment, forced displacement, and lack of critical resources. Prior to the October 2025 ceasefire, Oxfam and partners had reached more than 1.2 million people with humanitarian assistance despite severe access restrictions.

Ongoing Challenges

Progress has been fragile. On March 2, 2025, border crossings were closed to humanitarian and commercial supplies, ending the initial ceasefire period's aid surge. The October 2025 ceasefire brought another opening, with the UN humanitarian agency OCHA securing Israeli approval for 190,000 tonnes of food, shelter items, medicine, and other supplies.

However, a press release issued by 41 humanitarian organizations operating in Gaza urged the Government of Israel to uphold its commitments under the ceasefire. Between October 10 and 21, 2025, 17 international NGOs had urgent shipments of aid denied entry, with 94% of all rejections given to international NGOs. Nearly $50 million in humanitarian supplies remain stuck at crossings and warehouses.

In December 2025, Israel announced the suspension of permits for 37 non-governmental and aid organizations—including Action Against Hunger, Medecins Sans Frontieres, Mercy Corps, Norwegian Refugee Council, and Relief International—from operating in Gaza.

What's Needed

WFP has stated it needs US$300 million to meet urgent needs over six months and could send around 1,600 trucks of food (30,200 tons) each month to facilitate distributions for over 1 million people—if access is sustained. The humanitarian plan aims to provide food assistance for 2.1 million people, with in-kind rations, bakery and kitchen support, livelihood restoration for herders and fishers, and cash aid for 200,000 families.

WHO has facilitated medical evacuations—55 patients and 158 companions to Jordan, United Kingdom, Spain, and Turkey since the October 2025 ceasefire—but thousands more need evacuation. The IRC and its partners continue delivering critical aid, while UNRWA remains the backbone of humanitarian operations despite funding and operational challenges.

The ceasefires demonstrated what's possible: when access opens, aid can flow at scale. But sustaining that access remains the central challenge. As the World Food Programme notes: "WFP calls for sustained opening of humanitarian corridors" as the essential condition for preventing further catastrophe.

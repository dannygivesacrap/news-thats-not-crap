Baby KJ: World's First Personalized CRISPR Gene Therapy Success

By Gene Splice - January 29, 2026 - 5 min read

KJ Muldoon was born with CPS1 deficiency, a rare metabolic disorder that causes toxic ammonia to build up in the blood. About 50% of infants with the severe form die in infancy. In February 2025, he became the first person ever to receive a personalized CRISPR gene-editing therapy. Now he's home and thriving.

Sometimes science delivers miracles. On June 2, 2025, after 307 days at Children's Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP), KJ Muldoon went home. He was the first patient in history to be treated with a gene therapy designed specifically for his unique genetic mutation. And it worked.

KJ was born with severe carbamoyl phosphate synthetase 1 (CPS1) deficiency, a rare urea cycle disorder affecting approximately 1 in 1.3 million people worldwide. His body couldn't process protein properly, causing ammonia to accumulate to dangerous levels. When doctors first tested his blood, his ammonia levels exceeded 1,000 micromol/L - far above the normal range of 9-33.

"This case represents a blueprint for how we might approach rare genetic diseases in the future. Truly personalized medicine is now possible."

That assessment comes from researchers involved in KJ's treatment. The therapy was developed in a remarkable six months after KJ's birth - an unprecedented timeline for what is essentially a custom-built medicine.

The Treatment That Changed Everything

Researchers discovered that KJ had two specific CPS1 mutations - Q335X inherited from his father and E714X inherited from his mother. Dr. Rebecca Ahrens-Nicklas at CHOP and Dr. Kiran Musunuru at Penn Medicine designed a base editing therapy specifically targeting KJ's variant.

They named the therapy "kayjayguran abengcemeran" (or "k-abe" after KJ). It uses lipid nanoparticles to deliver the base editor to liver cells, where it corrects the faulty gene. Unlike traditional gene therapy, base editing doesn't cut the DNA - it chemically converts one letter of the genetic code to another, like a molecular spell-checker.

KJ received his first infusion on February 25, 2025, when he was six months old. He received follow-up doses in March and April 2025. The results were dramatic: his ammonia levels stabilized, he began tolerating increased dietary protein, and he needed less of the medication that had been keeping him alive.

What This Means for the Future

KJ's case demonstrates something profound: with modern base-editing technology, lipid-nanoparticle delivery, and rapid development pipelines, truly personalized single-patient therapies are now feasible. The treatment transformed his severe, neonatal-onset metabolic disorder into a much milder form of the disease.

Researchers involved in the case speak of this treatment as a "blueprint" for similar bespoke therapies that could be tailored rapidly for other rare metabolic or genetic diseases. There are thousands of such conditions, many affecting only a handful of patients worldwide.

KJ Muldoon has been recognized in Nature's 10, the prestigious list of people who shaped science in 2025. But for his family, the significance is simpler: they have a son who was supposed to die. He didn't. He's home.

A New Era of Medicine

The implications extend far beyond KJ's specific condition. The speed at which his therapy was developed - six months from diagnosis to first treatment - suggests a future where genetic diseases might be addressed almost as soon as they're identified.

"We're not just treating diseases anymore," the research team noted. "We're correcting the underlying genetic errors so the disease never fully develops. That's a fundamentally different kind of medicine."

For a baby who spent his first 307 days in the hospital, the future now stretches out wide and full of possibility. KJ Muldoon is alive because scientists built a treatment that had never existed before, specifically for him. That's what personalized medicine looks like.

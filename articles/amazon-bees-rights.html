Peru's Stingless Bees Become First Insects With Legal Rights

By Polly Nation - January 29, 2026 - 4 min read

In a decision that's creating quite a buzz in legal circles, Peru's Satipo province has granted legal rights to stingless bees - making them the first insects in history to receive such protection. And no, this isn't a fever dream.

On October 21, 2025, the provincial municipality of Satipo approved Ordinance No. 33, declaring the rights of stingless bees within the territory of the Avireri-Vraem Biosphere Reserve. It's the kind of sentence that sounds made up until you realize it represents a genuine legal milestone: the first time any government has recognized the rights of an insect.

Satipo is the largest and easternmost province in Peru's department of Junin. The Avireri-Vraem Biosphere Reserve, established by UNESCO in 2021, encompasses nearly 16,000 square miles of diverse ecosystems stretching from the Amazon rainforest to the Andes mountains. And now, its stingless bees have legal standing.

"The legal rights of stingless bees have now been approved in two local laws in Peru - this is just the beginning of global recognition for these essential pollinators."

Those words come from Rosa Vasquez Espinoza, founder of Amazon Research Internacional. The declaration was developed in collaboration with the Earth Law Center and EcoAshaninka, an organization representing around 8,000 people from 25 Indigenous Ashaninka, Machiguenga, and Kakinte communities.

What Does "Legal Rights" Mean for a Bee?

Under the new ordinance, meliponines (native stingless bees) have legal rights to exist, regenerate, and maintain healthy populations within their natural environment, free of harm from invasive species or human activity. They also have the right to have their habitat restored where it has been destroyed and to be defended when their rights are violated.

Native stingless bees including Melipona eburnea, Tetragonisca angustula, Melipona illota, and Melipona grandis are currently threatened by deforestation, pesticides, habitat loss, and climate change. This legal recognition gives Indigenous communities and conservationists new tools to fight back.

"This achievement is already generating global impact," noted the Earth Law Center. "Inspired by this milestone, the global movements Avaaz and Bee:wild have joined forces - mobilizing Avaaz's 70 million members - to call for the protection of stingless bees across all of Peru. Their petition has already gathered more than 335,000 signatures."

Why Stingless Bees Matter

While European honeybees get most of the press, stingless bees are ecological heavyweights in their own right. They're primary pollinators for many tropical plants, including crops like cacao, coffee, and tropical fruits. Without them, large swaths of Amazon biodiversity would collapse.

Indigenous communities have cultivated these bees for thousands of years. The practice, called meliponiculture, involves maintaining colonies in log hives for their honey, which has medicinal properties and a distinctive tangy flavor prized throughout South America.

Unlike their stinging cousins, these bees are remarkably gentle. Their defense mechanisms involve swarming and getting tangled in hair - annoying but not terrifying. This makes them ideal partners for the Indigenous communities who keep them.

A Precedent That's Already Spreading

The movement isn't limited to Satipo. According to Vasquez Espinoza, the legal rights of stingless bees have now been approved in two local laws in Peru - in both the province of Satipo (central Peru) and the province of Nauta (north Peru).

The campaign engaged over 650 community members (60% women, 10% youth), established 11 bee sanctuaries including two in Indigenous schools, and now protects 22 million bees across 300,000 square meters of rainforest.

For the Indigenous communities who have protected these bees for millennia, the legal recognition represents validation of traditional knowledge that Western law is finally beginning to understand.

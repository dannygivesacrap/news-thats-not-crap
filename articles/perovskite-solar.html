New Solar Cells Hit 34% Efficiency

By Sunny Watts · January 29, 2026 · 4 min read

For years, silicon solar panels have been stuck at around 22% efficiency—turning roughly a fifth of sunlight into electricity. A new generation of tandem cells combining silicon with perovskite has now smashed through that barrier, with LONGi achieving a certified 34.85% efficiency in April 2025.

The record, certified by the US National Renewable Energy Laboratory, represents a major leap over current commercial panels. Chinese solar producer JinkoSolar followed closely with a 34.76% power conversion efficiency for a perovskite-silicon tandem solar cell, certified by China's National PV Metric & Testing Center.

"Going from 22% to nearly 35% doesn't sound like much until you realize what it means in practice," solar researchers explain. "A rooftop that previously needed 20 panels now needs 13. The cost per watt drops dramatically. This is a really big deal."

"We're not incrementally improving silicon technology. We're adding a whole new layer that harvests light silicon can't even see. It's like giving solar panels superpowers."

That's how Oxford PV's CEO Chris Case describes the technology. The secret is the "tandem" approach: a perovskite layer on top of a silicon layer, with each material optimized to capture different wavelengths of light.

How Tandem Cells Work

Sunlight is a mix of wavelengths, from infrared to ultraviolet. Silicon is good at absorbing some of these wavelengths but essentially blind to others. When photons it can't use hit a silicon cell, their energy just converts to heat—wasted.

Perovskites—a class of crystalline materials that can be tuned to absorb specific wavelengths—solve this problem. In a tandem cell, the perovskite layer sits on top and captures high-energy blue and ultraviolet light. The light it doesn't absorb passes through to the silicon layer below, which captures the lower-energy red and infrared light.

The result is a panel that harvests a much larger portion of the solar spectrum. The theoretical efficiency limit for tandem cells is around 43%, compared to 29% for silicon alone—meaning there's still plenty of room for improvement.

The Perovskite Revolution

Perovskites have been the hottest topic in solar research for a decade, but they had a reputation problem: they degraded quickly when exposed to heat, humidity, or just sunlight—not ideal qualities for something meant to sit on rooftops for 25 years.

That's finally changing. Recent advances in encapsulation and material formulation have dramatically improved perovskite stability. Oxford PV's cells are now warranted for the same 25-year lifespan as conventional panels, a milestone that seemed distant just a few years ago.

"The durability question has been answered," says Case. "We've had cells in outdoor testing for over five years with no significant degradation. The technology is ready for the real world."

Coming to a Rooftop Near You

Unlike some laboratory breakthroughs that take decades to commercialize, tandem cells are already entering production. Trina Solar achieved a certified 32.6% efficiency for an industrial-scale tandem solar cell using a 210 mm half-cut format, while a standard-size tandem module delivered a peak power output of 865 W.

Researchers have also demonstrated a certified 33.6%-efficient flexible perovskite/crystalline silicon tandem solar cell with a record open-circuit voltage of 2.015 V—opening possibilities for applications where rigid panels won't work.

The implications for the energy transition are significant. Higher efficiency means solar becomes competitive in more locations—including cloudier, less sunny regions where it previously struggled. It means the same amount of land can generate more power. And it means rooftop solar can more easily cover a household's entire electricity needs.

"Every efficiency point matters. At nearly 35%, we're in a completely different ballpark than we were five years ago. The single-junction perovskite record now stands at 27.0%, certified by NREL. And researchers aren't done yet—forty percent is the next target."

Iron-Air Batteries Can Store Power for 100 Hours

By Gusty Winds · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

The biggest challenge with renewable energy has always been storage: the sun doesn't always shine, the wind doesn't always blow, but people always want to turn on lights. A new generation of batteries made from iron, water, and air might finally solve this problem—and at a fraction of the cost of lithium.

Form Energy has demonstrated something the energy industry has been chasing for decades: a grid-scale battery that can store electricity for 100 hours at a fraction of lithium-ion costs. In late 2024, the company's iron-air battery system successfully completed UL9540A safety testing—demonstrating no flame or thermal event propagation, no uncontrolled heating, no thermal runaway, and no fire.

The secret ingredient? Rust. During discharge, the battery absorbs oxygen from the air, which converts iron pellets into rust and releases energy. To charge, an electrical current converts the rust back into metallic iron and the battery releases oxygen. It's elegant, it's cheap, and it works.

"We're essentially reversing rusting. Charge the battery, you un-rust the iron. Use the battery, it rusts again. It's a chemical reaction humanity has known about for millennia—we just found a way to make it useful."

That's the core principle behind Form Energy's technology. In 2025, the company raised over $405 million—including investments from GE Vernova, Breakthrough Energy Ventures, and Energy Impact Partners—to bring this technology to commercial scale. Great River Energy and Form Energy broke ground on the Cambridge Energy Storage Project, a 1.5 MW / 150 MWh pilot project in Cambridge, Minnesota, expected to be operational by late 2025.

Why Duration Matters

Lithium-ion batteries are great at many things. They power our phones, our electric cars, and increasingly our homes. But they're fundamentally designed for short-duration storage—typically 4-6 hours. That works fine for smoothing out daily solar peaks or providing backup power during brief outages.

The problem comes when you need to bridge longer gaps. What happens when it's cloudy for three days straight? What about the week-long cold snap when heating demand peaks but solar output craters? What about seasonal variations—storing summer sun for winter use?

"Four-hour storage is a Band-Aid," explains Dr. Yet-Ming Chiang, an MIT professor who co-founded Form Energy. "To actually run a grid on renewables, you need storage that can ride through multi-day weather events. That's what 100-hour storage unlocks."

The Chemistry of Rust

The iron-air battery works through a process called "reversible rusting." When the battery discharges, iron pellets are exposed to air. The iron reacts with oxygen to form iron oxide—rust—and releases electrons in the process. Those electrons flow through a circuit to do useful work, like powering your house.

When you charge the battery, you reverse the process. Electricity splits the rust back into iron and oxygen. The oxygen vents away, and you're left with fresh iron pellets ready to rust again.

The reaction is slow, which is why these batteries aren't suitable for high-power applications like cars. But for grid storage, where you're smoothly charging and discharging over many hours, slow is fine. What matters is cost and capacity—and iron-air excels at both.

Made From Dirt Cheap Materials

Here's where iron-air batteries really shine: the main ingredients are iron (one of the most abundant elements on Earth), water, and air. There's no lithium, no cobalt, no nickel—just materials available everywhere on the planet.

Lithium-ion technology's primary constraint is its duration. While effective for short power bursts lasting between two and four hours, lithium-ion becomes economically unfeasible when attempting to bridge multi-day gaps in renewable generation. Iron-air batteries are capable of storing energy for over 100 hours—enabling wind and solar to keep the lights on even when the sun doesn't shine or the wind doesn't blow for days.

"We're not competing with lithium-ion—we're complementing it. Lithium handles short-duration, high-power applications. We handle the long-duration stuff. Together, you can build a fully renewable grid."

Utilities are taking notice. Form Energy is fulfilling orders for major utilities, including Xcel Energy and Georgia Power, with projects expected to come online in late 2025 and 2026. Berkshire Hathaway Energy-owned utility PacifiCorp is planning to install 3,073MW of iron-air battery storage by 2045, with 605MW of 100-hour iron air battery storage planned by 2032. With no heavy or rare-earth metals and approximately 80% of all components sourced domestically from within the United States, Form's battery provides a sustainable solution for grid security.

Form Energy has operationalized its commercial-scale factory in Weirton, West Virginia, on the site of a former steel mill. For a technology based on making things rusty, that's not too shabby.

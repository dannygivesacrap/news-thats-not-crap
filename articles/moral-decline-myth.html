Research Confirms Society Is Not Getting Worse

By Mo Rality · January 29, 2026 · 4 min read

"Kids these days have no respect." "People used to be kinder." "Society is falling apart." Sound familiar? A landmark 2023 study published in Nature, analyzing data from over 12 million respondents across 60 countries and 70 years, reveals these complaints are as old as civilization—and equally unfounded in every era.

Researchers Adam Mastroianni at Columbia University and Daniel Gilbert at Harvard have finally put numbers to what philosophers have suspected for millennia: the belief in moral decline is a persistent illusion. People have been complaining that society is getting worse for as long as we've had records of people complaining about anything.

The study, published in June 2023 in Nature, showed that people in at least 60 nations around the world believe that morality is declining, that they have believed this for at least 70 years, and that they attribute this decline both to the decreasing morality of individuals as they age and to the decreasing morality of successive generations. However, people's reports of the morality of their contemporaries have not declined over time—suggesting that the perception of moral decline is an illusion.

"Ask people whether their contemporaries are kind, and they'll say yes. Ask whether people used to be kinder, and they'll also say yes. Both can't be true—but people believe both anyway."

That's Dr. Adam Mastroianni explaining the paradox at the heart of the research. As he told the Harvard Gazette: "Moral breakdown is a fake problem"—a bold claim backed by one of the largest datasets ever assembled on the topic.

The BEAM Mechanism

Why does nearly everyone, everywhere, believe society is getting worse when it's not? The researchers identified a mechanism they call BEAM—Biased Exposure and Memory—that creates the illusion through two interlocking cognitive phenomena.

The first phenomenon, known as biased exposure, relates to how negative information disproportionately captures human attention and is more likely to be broadcast to others. News reports crimes, not kindnesses. Social media amplifies outrage, not gratitude. Our information diet is skewed toward the negative, so we assume the world itself is skewed.

The second phenomenon, biased memory, pertains to how recollections fade over time. Specifically, the negativity of negative information fades faster than the positivity of positive information. The annoying coworker from 2005 fades while fond memories of old friendships remain vivid. We compare an idealized past to a warts-and-all present.

"Put these together and you get a perfect recipe for the illusion of decline," explains Mastroianni. "You remember the past as better than it was, you perceive the present as worse than it is, and the gap between them feels like moral collapse."

Universal Across Age and Politics

One might expect older people to complain more about declining morals—after all, they've been alive longer and seen more change. But as Mastroianni notes: "If you ask people about decline over their entire lives, older people do report more, but they've been alive longer. Older and younger people perceive the same rate of decline."

Age and political affiliations do not seem to be significant factors in this shared perception. Contrary to expectations, both older and younger generations, and people across the political spectrum, expressed concerns about the state of morality. The illusion appears to be a universal feature of human psychology, transcending culture, politics, and era.

The study was successfully replicated in 2024, with Mastroianni confirming via email: "We are pleased to see these effects replicate." The findings have held up to scrutiny, reinforcing confidence that we're observing a genuine cognitive illusion rather than a statistical artifact.

The Political Danger

Believing society is collapsing isn't just wrong—it's harmful. As the researchers note, the project "underscores the political danger of romanticizing the past. Aspiring despots can and do prey upon declinist nostalgia, and the citizenry appears ready to squander precious resources on it."

The belief in moral decline can become self-fulfilling. If you think people are selfish, you'll be less likely to cooperate. If you think kindness is dying, you might not bother being kind yourself. The illusion can poison the well of social trust.

The antidote, the researchers suggest, is simply awareness. Knowing that this illusion exists—that it's a quirk of how our brains process information rather than a reflection of reality—can help us resist it. The next time someone says "people used to be better," you can smile knowingly. People said that in 1826, too. And 1526. And probably 526.

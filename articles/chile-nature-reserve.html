Chile Creates Cape Froward National Park: Tompkins Conservation's Final Gift

By Pat Agonia · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

At the southernmost tip of mainland South America, Chile has created its 47th national park—Cape Froward National Park—protecting 315,000 acres of pristine wilderness donated by Tompkins Conservation. This is the culmination of the largest private land donation for conservation in history.

Cape Froward—the true southern end of the continental Americas—has always been a place that seemed to reject human ambition. The weather is brutal, with horizontal rain, fierce winds, and temperatures that can swing wildly within hours. The Strait of Magellan churns nearby. The ancient forests are so dense that early explorers compared them to impenetrable green walls.

For centuries, people looked at this landscape and went elsewhere. Now, thanks to the conservation vision of the late Doug Tompkins and his wife Kristine, this wilderness will remain wild forever.

"This represents the completion of Doug's dream—a network of wild parklands stretching from the tip of Patagonia to the heart of Chile. These lands belong to all of humanity now."

Those words come from Kristine Tompkins, president of Tompkins Conservation, at the park's official dedication ceremony on January 15, 2026. Doug Tompkins, co-founder of The North Face and Esprit, died in a kayaking accident in 2015, but his conservation legacy continues to grow.

What Lives at the End of the World?

Quite a lot, as it turns out. The Cape Froward region is home to huemul deer (Chile's national animal, endangered), southern elephant seals, South American sea lions, and Magellanic penguins. Pumas prowl the forests. Andean condors ride the thermals. Humpback whales and orcas patrol the frigid waters of the strait.

The forests themselves are remarkable—ancient stands of lenga, coigue, and canelo trees that have adapted to some of the harshest conditions on Earth. Many of these trees are over 500 years old, gnarled survivors of storms that would flatten forests anywhere else. Below them, the ground is carpeted with mosses, lichens, and ferns in an explosion of green.

"This ecosystem has been essentially undisturbed for millennia," explains Dr. Barbara Saavedra, director of the Wildlife Conservation Society's Chile program. "It's one of the last places on Earth where you can experience what temperate rainforest looked like before humans shaped the landscape."

The Tompkins Legacy

Doug and Kristine Tompkins began purchasing land in Chile and Argentina in the 1990s, initially facing fierce criticism from locals who suspected the wealthy Americans of ulterior motives. But over three decades, they systematically acquired, restored, and donated over 2 million acres across both countries—an area larger than Yellowstone.

Their approach was distinctive: buy private land, remove fences and livestock, restore native wildlife, then donate the land to become national parks. In Chile alone, Tompkins Conservation has helped create or expand eleven national parks.

"They literally bought wilderness and gave it away," says Maisa Rojas, Chile's Minister of the Environment. "It sounds simple, but no one had ever done it at this scale. They changed what people think is possible in conservation."

Chile's Conservation Surge

Cape Froward is part of a broader Chilean conservation push. The country has now protected over 21% of its land area and is working toward protecting 30% of its ocean by 2030. The Route of Parks—a 1,700-mile scenic highway connecting seventeen national parks—has become a major tourism draw.

For visitors willing to make the journey, Cape Froward offers something increasingly rare: true wilderness. Getting there requires a boat ride through some of the world's most dangerous waters, followed by a trek through dense forest with no established trails.

Which, honestly, sounds kind of perfect.

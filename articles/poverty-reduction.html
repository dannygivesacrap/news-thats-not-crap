Global Poverty Continues Decline Despite Slowdown

By Penny Less · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

The World Bank's latest poverty data tells a complicated story: global extreme poverty is declining, but far more slowly than needed. Based on nowcasted estimates, extreme poverty is projected to fall from 10.3 percent in 2024 to 10.1 percent in 2025—progress, yes, but the 2020s may still become a "lost decade" in the fight against destitution.

We're conditioned to expect either doom or triumph in poverty coverage. The reality is messier. The long-term trend is genuinely remarkable: in 1990, roughly 36% of humanity lived in extreme poverty. Today, that figure hovers around 10%. But progress has slowed dramatically since the pandemic, and the goal of ending extreme poverty by 2030 is now definitively out of reach.

The World Bank recently updated its poverty line from $2.15 to $3.00 per day, reflecting new purchasing power parity calculations. Under this revised threshold, 839 million people lived in extreme poverty in 2024—still an enormous number, but down from nearly 2 billion three decades ago.

"Global poverty reduction has slowed to a near standstill, raising concerns that 2020–30 will be a lost decade. But a lost decade isn't the same as reversal. The trajectory still matters."

Those words capture the World Bank's sobering assessment from its 2024 "Poverty, Prosperity, and Planet Report." Progress continues, but at a pace that will leave roughly 9% of the global population—an estimated 793 million people—in extreme poverty by 2030, far from the UN's goal of eradication.

The Geography of Poverty

The regional disparities are stark. Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for 16% of the world's population but 67% of people living in extreme poverty. The poverty rate in the region stands at 46%—nearly half of all people. The Middle East and North Africa region has the second-highest rate, with around 1 in 8 people in extreme poverty.

By 2025, more than three-quarters of the global extreme poor will live in Sub-Saharan Africa or in fragile and conflict-affected countries. This geographic concentration presents both a challenge and an opportunity: resources and attention can be targeted where they're needed most.

"The upward revision of extreme poverty numbers stems primarily from an increase in the extreme poverty rate of Sub-Saharan Africa," the World Bank notes in its September 2025 update. Addressing poverty now means, in large part, addressing the specific challenges facing this region.

The Pandemic's Long Shadow

COVID-19 pushed an estimated 100 million people into extreme poverty, erasing years of hard-won progress. While much of that damage has been repaired, the crisis revealed how fragile development gains can be. Economic shocks, climate disasters, and conflict continue to threaten vulnerable populations.

The International Labour Organization (ILO) calls attention to "those left behind"—the people structural barriers prevent from participating in economic growth. As the ILOSTAT report notes, simply growing the economy isn't enough when employment remains informal, wages stay stagnant, and social protection is absent.

Still, the number of extremely poor people is projected to decline from 831 million in 2025 to 793 million in 2030. That's roughly 40 million fewer people in desperate poverty—progress worth acknowledging even as we recognize it's not enough.

The Long View

Step back far enough, and the story is unambiguously good. As Our World in Data documents, the share of humanity in extreme poverty has fallen from around 90% in 1820 to under 10% today. That's arguably the most important improvement in human welfare in history.

The challenge is sustaining progress in an era of slower growth, climate disruption, and geopolitical instability. The World Bank's revised projection—9% global poverty in 2030, rising again afterward—is a warning, not a destiny. What happens depends on policy choices about investment, aid, trade, and climate action.

"Eradicating extreme poverty by 2030 appears highly unlikely," the UN's SDG Indicators report states plainly. But unlikely isn't impossible, and the trajectory isn't fixed. We know what works: economic opportunity, social protection, education, healthcare, climate resilience. The question is whether the political will exists to apply those lessons at scale.

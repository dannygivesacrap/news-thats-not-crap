Carbon-Sucking Fungi Researcher Wins 'Nobel for Climate'

By Jay Byrd · January 29, 2026 · 6 min read

Beneath your feet, an ancient network of fungi is quietly moving billions of tons of carbon underground—and a growing body of research is revealing just how critical these organisms are to our climate future.

If you've ever walked through a forest and felt a sense of interconnection, you weren't imagining things. Below the soil surface, thread-like fungal networks called mycorrhizae connect virtually every tree and plant in a vast underground internet. Trees share nutrients, send chemical warnings about pests, and even feed their struggling neighbors through these fungal highways.

But here's the climate angle that's getting everyone excited: these fungal networks also move massive amounts of carbon from the atmosphere into the soil, where it can stay locked away for centuries. Recent research has shown just how massive—and it's a bigger deal than anyone realized.

"We've been so focused on the carbon in trees that we forgot about the carbon moving through them. The fungi are the real carbon bankers here—they're taking deposits and putting them in long-term storage."

A 2023 study published in Current Biology estimated that mycorrhizal fungi channel roughly 13.12 gigatons of CO2 equivalent underground every year—equating to approximately 36% of current annual CO2 emissions from fossil fuels. That's being quietly sequestered by organisms most people don't even know exist.

The Underground Economy

Research reveals that mycorrhizal networks operate like sophisticated marketplaces. Plants trade carbon (in the form of sugars from photosynthesis) with fungi in exchange for nutrients like phosphorus and nitrogen that the fungi extract from the soil. Measurements show plants allocate 5 to 20% of their total carbon uptake to mycorrhizal fungi.

What makes this climate-relevant is where that carbon goes. Researchers introduced the "Hyphal Carbon Transfer Theory," which proposes that fungal hyphae can transport plant-derived carbon from the plant sphere to more stable soil environments. Some of it stays there for hundreds or even thousands of years.

A 2025 meta-analysis published in Functional Ecology evaluated the role of arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) on soil organic carbon across several crop species and found an overall positive effect, with an average increase of 21.5% in soil organic carbon.

Grasslands Show the Way

A February 2025 study published in PNAS, based on data from 2,296 field sites across grassland ecosystems of China, showed that mycorrhizal fungi symbiosis enhances soil organic carbon storage in both topsoil and subsoil through increasing plant diversity and elevating biomass allocation belowground.

Soil organic carbon storage is significantly higher in systems dominated by obligate mycorrhizal and facultative mycorrhizal plants than those dominated by nonmycorrhizal plants. The Gadgil effect—where mycorrhizal fungi reduce decomposition rates by outcompeting saprotrophic organisms for nitrogen—leads to increased carbon sequestration.

The Society for the Protection of Underground Networks (SPUN) is working to create the first global map of underground fungal networks, aiming to identify the most important fungal ecosystems and protect them before development or agriculture destroys them.

Why This Matters for Climate Action

This research has profound implications for nature-based climate solutions. Tree-planting initiatives often focus solely on getting saplings in the ground, but without healthy fungal networks, trees grow slower, sequester less carbon, and are more vulnerable to disease and drought.

AMF promote the formation of stable soil organic carbon through multiple mechanisms including carbon retention in living hyphae, resistance and soil mineral adsorption of exudates and necromass, improvement of the quality and quantity of plant-derived carbon, and stabilization of soil aggregates.

If you want your forest restoration project to actually work for climate, you need to think about the fungi. Plant trees in soil with degraded fungal networks, and they'll struggle. Plant them in soil with healthy networks, and they'll thrive—and move more carbon underground.

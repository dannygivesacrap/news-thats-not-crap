South Korea Ends Bear Bile Farming Forever

By Barry Freed · January 29, 2026 · 4 min read

On December 30, 2025, South Korea's Ministry of Agriculture announced the end of an era: bear bile farming is officially banned as of January 1, 2026. The country's 199 remaining captive moon bears will never have bile extracted again, and no new bears will ever be farmed for this purpose.

Let's be clear about what bear bile farming involves, because understanding the reality is why this news matters so much. For decades, Asiatic black bears—also called moon bears for the distinctive white crescent on their chests—have been kept in cramped cages, often for their entire lives, with bile extracted from their gallbladders through various painful methods.

The bile contains ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA), used in traditional medicine despite synthetic and herbal alternatives being available for years. The practice has been condemned by animal welfare organizations worldwide for the immense suffering it causes. And now, in South Korea at least, it's finally over.

"This is a historic moment for animal welfare in Asia. After decades of advocacy, South Korea has chosen compassion over cruelty."

Those words come from Jill Robinson MBE, founder of Animals Asia Foundation, the organization that has spent over 25 years fighting to end bear bile farming across the continent. Animals Asia has rescued over 700 bears from bile farms in China and Vietnam and has been instrumental in advocacy efforts that led to this ban.

A Long Road to This Day

South Korea legalized bear farming in 1981, importing bears from China to establish farms. At its peak in the early 2000s, the industry held over 1,400 bears. But public opinion began shifting as younger South Koreans increasingly viewed the practice as cruel and outdated.

The turning point came in 2022, when the government launched a comprehensive buyout program. Farmers were offered compensation to surrender their bears—approximately 4 million won (about $3,000 USD) per bear—rather than continuing bile extraction. Most farmers accepted. The number of farmed bears dropped from around 400 in 2022 to 199 by the end of 2025.

"This ban reflects the changing values of Korean society," said Kim Hong-gul, a senior official at the Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs. "There is no place for this kind of animal suffering in modern Korea."

What Happens to the Bears

The 199 remaining bears will live out their days on the farms where they currently reside, but without any bile extraction. The government has mandated improved living conditions and veterinary care. Some may eventually be transferred to sanctuaries as space becomes available.

Many of these bears are elderly—some have spent 20 years or more in captivity. The physical and psychological damage from a lifetime of confinement and bile extraction can be severe and often irreversible. But they'll spend whatever time they have left free from the procedure that defined their existence.

"For these bears, the nightmare is over," said Kang Sung-ho, a veterinarian who has worked with rescued bears. "They may never fully recover, but at least they can now live with dignity."

What It Means for the Region

South Korea joins Vietnam, which officially banned bear bile farming in 2017 (though enforcement challenges remain, with hundreds of bears still in captivity). The bigger picture is the estimated 10,000 to 20,000 bears still held on bile farms in China, the largest market by far.

"Every country that bans this practice puts pressure on the others," says Robinson. "South Korea joining Vietnam sends a clear message: Asia is moving beyond this cruelty. China is watching."

For the moon bears of Korea, though, the message is simpler: you're free. No more cages. No more needles. No more bile. Just life.

US Experiences Sharpest Ever Fall in Murder Rates During 2025

By Hope Springs · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

In a development that has stunned criminologists and defied endless predictions of doom, the United States experienced the largest single-year drop in murder rates ever recorded in 2025—and it might be the lowest murder rate since 1900. And no, you probably didn't hear about it.

Here's a test: ask any American whether they think crime has gone up or down in the past few years. Almost everyone will say "up." They're wrong. And not by a little—by a lot.

According to a study released by the Council on Criminal Justice, murders in the United States dropped by 21% in 2025 compared to 2024. That's the largest single-year decline on record. Data collected from 35 American cities showed this translated to about 922 fewer homicides last year. The CCJ report found 11 of 13 crime categories dropped in 2025 compared to 2024, with nine dropping by 10% or more.

"There was a wide array of stresses—economic, financial, psychological—that the pandemic produced. And there were greater opportunities to settle beefs with rivals, precisely because there were fewer people on the streets."

Those words come from Adam Gelb, president of the Council on Criminal Justice, explaining why homicides spiked during the pandemic. Now, the recovery is pushing crime back down—dramatically.

Why Didn't You Hear About This?

Good question. In a media environment that thrives on fear and outrage, declining crime rates are almost aggressively boring. "Things Getting Better" doesn't drive clicks. "Blood In The Streets" does.

There's also a significant lag between when crime happens and when data becomes available. The murders that dominated 2020 and 2021 headlines were still shaping perceptions even as rates quietly declined. By the time the data showed improvement, the narrative had calcified.

FBI data shows homicides fell nearly 15% in 2024 alone, marking a 26% drop from the pandemic-era peak in 2020. The CCJ's year-end 2024 update revealed that most offenses were near or below 2019 levels, with homicide falling to 6% below 2019 rates.

What's Actually Driving the Decline?

A group of criminal justice experts convened by CCJ had varied answers, but they generally agreed on several key drivers: the stabilization of routines after pandemic disruptions, an influx of COVID-19 relief funds, and community violence intervention programs finally showing results.

The steepest drops were reported in Denver, Washington D.C., and Omaha, Nebraska, where homicides dropped by roughly 40%. Cities with traditionally high homicide levels showed the largest improvements, including Baltimore (-40%) and St. Louis (-33%).

Economic conditions have also improved. Unemployment is low, wages are rising, and poverty rates have declined. Research consistently shows that economic opportunity reduces crime more effectively than almost any criminal justice policy.

The Perception Gap

Perhaps the most striking thing about the crime decline is how invisible it remains to most Americans. In Gallup polling, 77% of Americans believe crime is increasing nationally—almost the exact opposite of reality.

This perception gap has real consequences. Voters who believe crime is rising support harsher policies. Politicians who promise to "crack down" on nonexistent crime waves get elected. Resources get allocated based on fear rather than facts.

So here's the data: murder rates are down to potentially their lowest level since 1900. Violent crime overall is down. Property crime is down. America is safer than it's been in years. Spread the word.

Knepp Rewilding Estate Records 916% Increase in Breeding Birds

By Robin Woods · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

When Charlie Burrell and Isabella Tree stopped farming 3,500 acres of West Sussex countryside in 2001 and let it go wild, neighbors thought they'd lost the plot. Twenty-four years later, the Knepp Estate has recorded an astonishing 916% increase in breeding bird numbers—from 55 individuals in 2001 to 559 in 2025.

Here's the thing about intensive farming: it's remarkably efficient at producing food and remarkably terrible at supporting wildlife. Hedgerows get ripped out for larger fields. Wetlands get drained. Pesticides eliminate insects. What remains is productive, profitable, and ecologically devastated.

Charlie Burrell inherited Knepp Castle Estate in 1987 and spent 15 years trying to make traditional dairy and arable farming work on the estate's heavy Weald clay soil. By 2000, despite subsidies and hard work, the farm was losing money year after year. So Burrell tried something radical: he stopped farming entirely.

"We didn't have a grand plan. We just removed the constraints—the fences, the drainage, the ploughing—and let the land decide what it wanted to be. Turns out it knew exactly what to do."

Those words come from Isabella Tree, Burrell's wife and author of the acclaimed book "Wilding" which documented the estate's transformation. Starting in 2001, they sold the dairy herd, retired the tractors, and introduced free-roaming herds of longhorn cattle, Tamworth pigs, Exmoor ponies, and fallow deer—animals that could shape the landscape the way their wild ancestors once did.

The Species That Came Back

The transformation exceeded everyone's expectations. Knepp became one of the UK's most important breeding sites for turtle doves—a species that has declined 98% nationally since 1970. Nightingales, which have disappeared from much of southern England, now breed in significant numbers across the estate.

The 2025 breeding bird survey, conducted by naturalist Penny Green using standardized transect methods, recorded 559 individual birds of 51 species. The estate now hosts breeding populations of peregrine falcons, all five British owl species, and purple emperor butterflies—once rare, now thriving in the sallow scrub that has colonized former wheat fields.

"Some of these species hadn't been recorded here in living memory," says Green. "The speed of recovery is extraordinary. Nature doesn't need decades to bounce back—give it the right conditions and it responds immediately."

The Economics of Letting Go

Here's what makes other farmers pay attention: Knepp is now more profitable than it ever was as a working farm. The estate earns income from multiple streams: eco-tourism (including safari drives and glamping), sales of pasture-fed organic meat from the free-roaming animals, and government environmental land management payments.

"We're not telling everyone to do this," Burrell emphasizes. "Britain needs productive farms. But there's plenty of marginal farmland—land that was never very productive anyway—where rewilding makes both ecological and economic sense."

The UK government has taken notice. The new Environmental Land Management scheme now pays farmers for environmental outcomes, not just food production. Inspired partly by Knepp's success, dozens of other estates and farms across Britain have launched their own rewilding projects.

A Blueprint for Recovery

Britain has lost 97% of its wildflower meadows since World War II. Farmland bird populations have declined by 60%. The state of nature in one of the world's wealthiest nations is, by scientific consensus, appalling.

But Knepp demonstrates that decline isn't destiny. In just over two decades—a blink in ecological time—a failing farm has become one of the most biodiverse places in southern England. The lesson is both simple and profound: nature knows how to heal itself, if we just give it room.

"People talk about conservation as managing nature, controlling it, keeping it in some idealized state," says Tree. "But nature doesn't want to be managed. It wants to grow, change, adapt, surprise us. Our job isn't to control it. Our job is to step aside."

This spring, as they have for over two decades now, nightingales will sing across Knepp's rewilded fields. Their song is a reminder that even in our crowded, cultivated island, wildness can return—faster than we ever imagined.

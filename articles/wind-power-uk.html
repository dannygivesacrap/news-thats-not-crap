UCL Study: Wind Power Has Delivered 104 Billion Pounds in Net Benefits to the UK

By Gusty Winds · January 29, 2026 · 5 min read

Those giant spinny things? They're paying off spectacularly. A landmark study from University College London has calculated that UK wind power delivered a net benefit of 104 billion pounds between 2010 and 2023—and that's after accounting for all subsidies, infrastructure costs, and integration expenses.

The study, published in the journal Nature Energy and led by Professor Michael Grubb at UCL's Institute for Sustainable Resources, is the most comprehensive analysis yet of wind power's economic impact on the UK. And the numbers are staggering.

Over the 13-year period studied, UK wind generation reduced wholesale electricity prices significantly through what economists call the "merit order effect." When wind is blowing, expensive gas-fired power plants get pushed out of the generation stack, and the market-clearing price drops for everyone.

The Merit Order Effect

The mechanism behind these savings is well-understood but often overlooked. In wholesale electricity markets, power plants are stacked in order of their marginal operating costs, from cheapest (wind, solar, nuclear) to most expensive (gas). The price everyone pays is set by the most expensive plant needed to meet demand.

"Wind power isn't just displacing fossil fuels—it's reducing prices for all electricity. The benefits compound across the entire system."

Those words come from Professor Grubb, who led the UCL research team. "What we've shown is that the net benefit to UK consumers and the economy far exceeds what was spent on wind support policies. This wasn't just a good investment—it was an exceptional one."

The study found that during periods of high wind generation, wholesale electricity prices fell by an average of 22% compared to what they would have been with gas-only generation. During the 2022 energy crisis, when gas prices spiked to record levels, wind power provided crucial price relief.

Breaking Down the Numbers

The 104 billion pound figure represents net benefit—total savings minus all costs. The researchers accounted for Contracts for Difference payments (the main subsidy mechanism), grid integration costs, backup capacity requirements, and transmission upgrades. Even after all these costs, wind came out massively ahead.

The study also noted that benefits are accelerating. As older, more expensive wind contracts expire and are replaced by newer projects that require little or no subsidy, the net benefit is expected to grow substantially. Recent offshore wind auction prices have come in below wholesale electricity prices—meaning new wind farms will actively reduce bills rather than add to them.

"We're entering a phase where wind power actively pays money back to consumers," says Professor Grubb. "The economics have shifted fundamentally."

What This Means Going Forward

The UK government has set a target of 50 gigawatts of offshore wind by 2030, up from approximately 15 gigawatts today. If the UCL findings hold, this expansion could deliver hundreds of billions in additional net benefits over the coming decades.

The study also serves as a sharp rebuttal to claims that renewable energy is too expensive. For years, critics argued that wind subsidies were a burden on consumers. The UCL research shows the opposite: wind power has been a massive net positive for the UK economy.

Sometimes the technology that saves the planet also saves us money. What a concept.

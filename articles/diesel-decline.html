Diesel Cars Are Now Officially In "Terminal Decline"

By Ev Lution · January 29, 2026 · 4 min read

Pour one out for the diesel engine. Actually, don't—that would be an environmental hazard. New data from the European Automobile Manufacturers' Association confirms that diesel car sales have collapsed so dramatically that the technology is now in what industry analysts are calling "terminal decline."

Let's put some numbers on this obituary. In 2015, diesel cars accounted for over 50% of new car sales in Europe. By 2024, that share had fallen to just 14%. In the UK, diesel's market share dropped to around 5.3% in 2025—down from 7.5% in 2023. These aren't gradual declines. This is a collapse.

The trajectory is now unmistakable. According to Transport & Environment, diesel car sales in Europe are expected to fall below 10% market share by the end of 2025 and could effectively reach zero for new passenger vehicles by the early 2030s.

"Diesel had a good run, but the future is electric. The numbers aren't just trending that way—they've already arrived."

Those words come from Julia Poliscanova, senior director at Transport & Environment, the Brussels-based advocacy group that has been tracking European vehicle sales for over a decade. Even she seems a bit startled by how quickly things have moved.

What Killed Diesel?

Several things, really. Dieselgate was the turning point—Volkswagen's 2015 emissions cheating scandal shattered consumer trust and triggered a wave of regulations. Cities across Europe began implementing low-emission zones that banned older diesels from urban centers. Resale values cratered as buyers fled the market.

But the real killer was electric vehicles. Once EVs became genuinely competitive on price and range, diesel's main selling point—fuel efficiency—stopped mattering. Why worry about miles per gallon when you can skip the gas station entirely?

"The crossover happened faster than anyone predicted," says Dr. Colin McKerracher, head of transport analysis at BloombergNEF. "Five years ago, we thought diesel would have another decade. Instead, it's already functionally over in many markets."

The EV Takeover Continues

Norway, as always, is leading the charge. In 2025, over 90% of new car sales in Norway were battery-electric vehicles. Diesel accounted for less than 2%. The country has essentially skipped ahead to the future while the rest of Europe catches up.

But it's not just Norway anymore. Germany saw EV sales reach 25% market share in 2024. France hit 27%. The UK reached 23%. Even in traditionally diesel-heavy markets like Spain and Italy, EVs are now the fastest-growing segment.

The EU's 2035 ban on new internal combustion engine sales may turn out to be redundant—the market is moving faster than the regulations.

What It Means for the Climate

Road transport accounts for roughly 12% of global CO2 emissions and is one of the sectors where decarbonization has been hardest to achieve. The collapse of diesel and the rise of EVs represents one of the most significant decarbonization trends currently happening on the planet.

"Every diesel car that doesn't get sold is a win for air quality and climate," says Poliscanova. "We're watching a technological transition happen in real time, and it's happening faster than the pessimists said it could."

So goodbye, diesel. You served your purpose. But the future doesn't need you anymore.

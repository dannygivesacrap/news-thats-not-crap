Community Colleges Lead on Campus Sustainability

By Dean Slist · January 29, 2026 · 3 min read

Across Texas, community colleges are proving that you don't need a billion-dollar endowment to make meaningful progress on sustainability. From Austin Community College's zero-waste commitment to student-led projects diverting tens of thousands of pounds of waste statewide, two-year institutions are emerging as unexpected leaders in campus climate action.

The numbers from EcoRise's 2024-25 Texas Impact report are striking: across 653 schools, more than 61,000 students and 1,099 teachers took on real-world environmental challenges—diverting over 44,000 pounds of waste, saving 43,000 gallons of water, and creating 60,000 square feet of green space. Much of this work happened at community colleges and public schools with modest budgets and outsized ambition.

Austin Community College has set perhaps the most ambitious goals: becoming zero waste by 2040 and achieving climate neutrality by 2050. They're funding it through a sustainability fee of just $1 per semester credit hour—the average student pays $8 per semester, generating roughly $750,000 annually for energy and sustainability initiatives.

"Community colleges have been invisible in this space. But we serve students who'll be the electricians, HVAC technicians, and building managers implementing sustainability in the real world. Why wouldn't we be leaders?"

That sentiment echoes across the community college sustainability movement. A winter 2022 Student Voice survey by Inside Higher Ed and College Pulse found 81% of college students were at least somewhat worried about climate change, and 85% said it was at least somewhat important for their campus to prioritize sustainability. Community colleges are responding.

The Green Economy Pipeline

The Community College Research Center at Columbia University has been studying how two-year institutions can prepare students for high-quality jobs in the emerging green economy. In April 2024, CCRC hosted a half-day symposium at the AACC Annual Convening for community college, industry, and government leaders, spotlighting promising programs already in place.

Starting in October 2024, CCRC partnered with the University of Tennessee, Knoxville to conduct a one-year landscape study of training programs, releasing the Advanced Infrastructure, Energy, and Agriculture (AIREA) Data Explorer in September 2025 to map green workforce programs nationwide.

California's community colleges launched their Climate Fellows Program in September 2024, placing fellows at campuses across the state to advance climate action education and engagement. The fellows focus on four key areas: community engagement, curriculum development, workforce training, and campus facilities. One fellow, Tonya Huff at Riverside City College, serves as faculty chair of the RCC Sustainability Committee and co-advises the Student Sustainability Collective.

Student Creativity Drives Results

The Texas projects showcase remarkable student creativity: vermicomposting programs in San Antonio, sustainable food initiatives in Wimberley, and new recycling programs in the Rio Grande Valley. As EcoRise notes, "These hands-on experiences not only improve local environments but also cultivate the leadership and collaboration skills students need to thrive in a changing world."

AASHE's Sustainability Tracking, Assessment & Rating System (STARS) has certified 388 institutions with sustainability ratings, measuring performance across academics, operations, engagement, and planning. The 2025 Sustainable Campus Index, released in September, recognizes top performers in each category, including dedicated lists for community colleges (Associate/Short-cycle institutions).

One way institutions are encouraging student advocacy is through funding sustainable initiatives directly. The model pioneered at places like Austin Community College—small per-credit fees generating substantial pooled resources—is being replicated at two-year schools nationwide. It turns out that meaningful climate action doesn't require Harvard's $50 billion endowment. It requires students willing to show up, do the work, and hold their institutions accountable.
